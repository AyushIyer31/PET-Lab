The Problem
380 million tons.
Less than 10% recycled.

PET plastic is everywhere — water bottles, food packaging, polyester. It doesn't break down. It accumulates for centuries.

Mechanical recycling grinds plastic into lower-grade material. Enzymatic degradation breaks PET back to its original monomers — terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol — which can be repolymerised into virgin-grade plastic.

That circular loop is only possible with an enzyme that works. And right now, the best ones don't work hot enough.

Background
IsPETase can eat plastic.
But it falls apart at 40 °C.

In 2016, Yoshida et al. discovered Ideonella sakaiensis — a bacterium that lives on PET. Its enzyme IsPETase was the first found to degrade PET at room temperature.

The problem: IsPETase unfolds above ~40 °C. PET chains only become mobile enough for enzymatic attack above its glass transition temperature (~65–70 °C).

Every degree of thermal stability gained is a meaningful improvement in degradation rate. Getting from 40 °C to 70 °C is the difference between weeks and hours.

Our Approach
Predict which mutations
improve thermal stability.

Prior methods (FoldX, Rosetta) need a 3D crystal structure and expensive compute. We built a sequence-only ML model — paste in any FASTA, get predictions.

  • Train on 17,791 real experimental mutations from two curated databases
  • Predict ΔΔG (change in folding free energy) per mutation
  • Rank and combine into multi-mutant candidates
  • Correct for real assay conditions: salt, pH, Ca²⁺, temperature
  • Serve through a web app — no software install needed
Training Data
Only real experiments.
No synthetic data.

We made a deliberate choice: train only on measured ΔΔG values from wet lab experiments. No computationally predicted values — that would just be learning to copy another model's errors.

FireProtDB
3,438 mutations · curated single-point substitutions with experimental ΔΔG and exact assay conditions
ThermoMutDB
10,993 mutations · 249 protein families · temperature and pH metadata per record
Feature Engineering
76 numbers to describe
one amino acid swap.

Every mutation becomes a 76-dimensional vector — computed from the sequence and assay conditions alone. No crystal structure needed.

  • Physicochemical deltas (12) — change in hydrophobicity, volume, charge, flexibility, helix/sheet propensity + BLOSUM62
  • Structural context (9) — estimated RSA, secondary structure fractions, local sequence window
  • Assay conditions (2) — temperature and pH from the database record
  • Extended biochemical (18) — MW, H-bonds, pKa shift, aliphatic index, disorder, Cys distance
  • Cross-terms (8) — physically motivated products of condition × structure variables
The Model
Six models. Then a
meta-classifier on top.

No single algorithm consistently wins on tabular biological data. We train six independently tuned regressors and combine them — then add a second layer.

Physics Corrections
The ML model doesn't know
about salt or calcium.

Temperature and pH are in the training data, so the model learns those. But ionic strength and Ca²⁺ vary too little in our training set to learn from — so we add explicit physics.

Debye-Hückel (Ionic Strength)
Salt ions screen charge-charge interactions. At 500 mM NaCl vs. 50 mM, a surface charge mutation matters less because counterions partially cancel it out. We apply a screening correction only to surface-exposed charged residues (RSA > 0.25). Capped at ±0.15 score units.
Ca²⁺ Hill Equation (Cutinases)
LCC, TfCut2, Cut190 have a conserved Ca²⁺ binding site. Mutations changing chelating residues (Asp/Glu) alter Ca²⁺ affinity and therefore stability. We model this with a one-site Hill equation: f = [Ca²⁺]/(Kd + [Ca²⁺]), Kd = 0.5 mM.
Platform
A researcher pastes in
a sequence. Gets candidates.

The full system is a public web app. No software to install. Set your conditions, hit Optimize, get ranked mutations back in a few seconds.

  • Condition sliders — temperature, pH, ionic strength, Ca²⁺ concentration
  • Ranked candidate table — ΔΔG score, predicted ΔTm, activity score, literature support
  • 3D molecular viewer — IsPETase structure with mutation sites highlighted; animated guided tour
  • Literature cross-reference — auto-checks against FAST-PETase, LCC-ICCG, published variants
  • Explainability — feature attribution for every mutation, shows which factors drove the score
POST /optimize { "sequence": "MSGGSSGLPAL...", "target_temperature": 70.0, "ph": 8.0, "ionic_strength_mm": 150.0 } → ranked candidates in ~3 seconds
Honest Assessment
What we can't do yet,
and why it matters.

71.5% is a real number, not a polished one. Here's what's still missing:

  • No structural features — FoldX/Rosetta compute explicit van der Waals contacts from 3D coordinates. Our RSA is a sequence-only estimate. That's a real accuracy gap.
  • No PSSM profiles — evolutionary conservation (BLAST) would add ~2-4% but takes minutes per protein. Too slow for real-time inference. Pre-computing for common families is next.
  • Additivity assumption — multi-mutant scores assume ΔΔG values add up. That breaks down for nearby residues in the same structural element.
  • LOPO gap — 66.1% on unseen protein families vs. 71.5% overall. ~4% of accuracy is protein-family-specific, not universal mutation physics.

What could push it to 80%:

AlphaFold2 Predicted RSA
Replace sequence-estimated RSA with AlphaFold2 predicted structures. Estimated +3-4% accuracy. AlphaFold covers most known proteins now.
PSSM Conservation Features
Pre-computed BLAST profiles for common enzyme families. Estimated +2-3%. Already partially implemented — just needs the compute time.
ProDDG / S2648 Data
The S2648 dataset wasn't available in our current deployment. Adding it would give ~2,600 more training examples across new protein families.
Conclusion
Sequence in.
Ranked candidates out.

We built a full ML pipeline for protein stability prediction — from data curation to physics corrections to a working web app — using only sequence features.

The model reaches 71.5% binary accuracy and r = 0.636 Pearson correlation without a crystal structure. That's competitive with published sequence-only methods and useful as a first-pass filter: cut 10,000 possible mutations down to 50 candidates for wet lab validation.

The goal isn't to replace wet lab experiments — it's to make computational pre-screening accessible to any research group with a protein sequence and an internet connection.
