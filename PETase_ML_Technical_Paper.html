PETase-ML: Using Machine Learning to Engineer
More Effective Plastic-Degrading Enzymes

Los Altos Hacks Research Team

PET Enzyme Engineering Lab  ·  2026

Abstract Plastic pollution is a massive, urgent problem, and PET plastic is one of the hardest materials to break down. The bacterial enzyme IsPETase can degrade PET, but it unfolds at temperatures too low for industrial use. We built PETase-ML, a machine learning platform that predicts which mutations to a protein's amino acid sequence will make the enzyme more thermally stable. We trained an ensemble of six gradient-boosted models on 17,791 experimentally measured mutations from two public databases, then added a stacking meta-classifier that combines regression and direct classification signals. We also incorporated two physics-based corrections — one for salt concentration (Debye-Hückel theory) and one for calcium binding (Hill equation) — so predictions adjust for real industrial conditions, not just lab buffers. Our model reached 71.4% accuracy on binary stability classification with a Pearson correlation of r = 0.63. The platform is deployed as a web app with an interactive 3D molecular viewer and literature cross-referencing. We describe the full pipeline, what worked, what didn't, and where we think the model still has room to improve.

1. Introduction

1.1 Why This Problem

We picked this project because the numbers around plastic waste are genuinely staggering. About 380 million metric tons of plastic gets manufactured every year, and less than 10% is ever recycled. PET specifically — the polymer in most water bottles and food packaging — can persist in the environment for hundreds of years. It doesn't break down. It just accumulates.

In 2016, a research group in Japan discovered a bacterium called Ideonella sakaiensis that could actually live on PET as its main food source (Yoshida et al., 2016). Its primary enzyme, IsPETase, was the first enzyme found to meaningfully degrade PET at room temperature. That discovery kicked off a wave of work trying to make the enzyme better — faster, more stable, cheaper to produce.

The main bottleneck is temperature. IsPETase starts falling apart above roughly 40 °C, which is a problem because PET's polymer chains only become flexible enough for enzymatic attack above ~65 °C — its glass transition temperature. Getting the enzyme to work at 70 °C instead of 40 °C isn't just a marginal improvement; it's the difference between an enzyme that degrades a few percent of a PET sample in weeks versus one that can degrade 90% in a day.

Researchers have already made progress here. Lu et al. (2022) used machine learning to design FAST-PETase, a five-mutation variant that works at room temperature. Tournier et al. (2020) engineered LCC-ICCG, a thermophilic cutinase that degrades 90% of post-consumer PET in 10 hours at 72 °C. Both of those studies required either structural modeling with expensive software (Rosetta, FoldX) or large-scale wet lab screening. We wanted to ask: can a sequence-only machine learning model get close to that level of prediction without needing a crystal structure?

1.2 What We Built

PETase-ML has three parts. First, an ML prediction model trained on real experimental mutation data that predicts whether a given amino acid substitution will stabilise or destabilise a protein. Second, a set of physics-based adjustments that correct predictions based on the salt concentration and calcium content of the user's environment — because a mutation that helps in a low-salt lab buffer might behave differently in industrial wastewater. Third, a full web interface where a researcher can paste in any protein sequence, set their assay conditions, and get back a ranked list of candidate mutations with predicted stability scores, melting temperature changes, and cross-references to published experiments.

2. Training Data

2.1 Where the Data Came From

One of the first things we learned was that data quality matters more than data quantity. We decided early on to use only real experimental measurements — no computationally predicted stability values, no synthetic augmentation from Rosetta or FoldX. The reasoning was straightforward: a model trained on computational predictions would just be learning to mimic another model's errors, not real biophysics.

We drew from two databases. FireProtDB provided 3,438 curated mutations with experimentally measured ΔΔG values and the exact assay conditions (temperature and pH) used in the original experiments. ThermoMutDB contributed 10,993 more mutations across 249 distinct protein families. After merging and removing duplicates — same protein, same position, same wild-type and mutant amino acids — we had 8,925 unique mutations. We then removed 20 entries where |ΔΔG| exceeded 10 kcal/mol, because values that extreme almost always indicate measurement error or protein aggregation rather than real single-mutation effects. That left us with 8,905 clean training examples.

2.2 Doubling the Dataset for Free

Thermodynamics gives us a useful identity: the destabilisation caused by mutation A→B is equal and opposite to the stabilisation of the reverse mutation B→A. In equation form: 

DDG(A → B)  =  −DDG(B → A)

We used this to generate a reverse mutation for every entry in our dataset. After removing any reverse mutations that were already present in the original data, we ended up with 17,791 total samples. This is a legitimate augmentation — we're not guessing stability values, we're applying a thermodynamic identity that holds exactly in theory and approximately in practice. That distinction matters: the augmented examples carry real physical meaning.

Why near-equal class balance matters: After augmentation, we had 8,780 stabilising mutations (49.3%) and 9,011 destabilising (50.7%). This near-perfect balance means we don't need any resampling tricks, and accuracy is a valid metric — a model can't get to 70%+ just by predicting the majority class.

3. Feature Engineering

3.1 Representing a Mutation as a Number

The central challenge in this kind of problem is turning a biological event — "amino acid serine at position 121 was replaced by glutamate" — into something a gradient-boosted tree can work with. We can't feed it a protein sequence directly; we need numbers. We ended up with a 76-dimensional feature vector for each mutation, computed entirely from the amino acid sequence and the assay conditions. No crystal structure required.

That sequence-only constraint was a deliberate choice. Many of the most interesting proteins being studied today — including newly discovered plastic-degrading enzymes from environmental metagenomics — don't have crystal structures yet. A model that needs 3D coordinates is useless for those. Our model works on any protein you can express as a FASTA sequence.

4. The Prediction Model

4.1 Why an Ensemble?

No single gradient-boosted model consistently wins on this kind of structured biological data. Different algorithms make different bias-variance tradeoffs: XGBoost handles deep trees with strong regularisation, LightGBM is fast on the histogram approximation, CatBoost handles symmetric trees well. We found that averaging the outputs of six independently tuned models reduced error more reliably than trying to squeeze extra performance from any one of them. The MLP adds a completely different kind of inductive bias — a fully connected neural network that can learn arbitrary non-linear combinations — which helps even when its individual accuracy is lower than the tree models.

4.3 The Wide-Stack Meta-Classifier

One thing we noticed early on: the base models were all doing regression — predicting a continuous ΔΔG value, then thresholding at zero to get a binary stabilising/destabilising label. But minimising mean absolute error on ΔΔG isn't the same as maximising binary classification accuracy. You can have a model with great MAE that still gets the sign wrong on a lot of borderline mutations.

So we added three direct binary classifiers (XGBClassifier, LGBMClassifier, CatBoostClassifier) trained on the binary label directly. These optimise accuracy from the start. We then built a wide 9-feature meta-learner input from: the 6 regressor out-of-fold (OOF) predictions + the 3 classifier OOF probabilities. A CatBoost classifier meta-learner trained on this combined input picks up signal from both the regression and classification branches.

4.4 Leave-One-Protein-Out Validation

Standard 10-fold CV shuffles mutations randomly across folds, so the same protein can appear in both training and test. That's a problem because our model might learn protein-specific patterns (a specific protein family's average stability) rather than general mutation effects. Leave-one-protein-out (LOPO) CV fixes this by holding out every mutation from one protein at a time. It's a harder test, and the numbers reflect that:

  • LOPO MAE: 1.363 kcal/mol (vs. 1.25 in standard CV)
  • LOPO Pearson r: 0.448
  • LOPO Binary Accuracy: 66.0%

The drop from 70.4% to 66.0% tells us that about 4% of our accuracy comes from within-protein generalisation — the model picking up on a specific protein family's behaviour rather than universal mutation physics. We think that's actually a reasonable number given that protein families do have real shared behaviours that a model should capture.

5. Physics-Based Condition Corrections

5.1 Why Conditions Matter

A ΔΔG value measured in a 50 mM NaCl buffer at pH 7 is not the same as one measured in industrial wastewater with 500 mM NaCl at pH 9. Ionic strength changes how charged residues interact with each other. Calcium concentration matters for cutinase-family enzymes (like LCC) that have a calcium binding site critical for thermostability. Our model's training data includes temperature and pH as features, so it handles those natively. But ionic strength and calcium concentration required explicit physics models, because our training data doesn't have enough variation in those conditions to learn from.

5.2 Debye-Hückel Ionic Strength Correction

When you dissolve NaCl in water, the Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions form an ionic atmosphere around any charged surface residues, partially cancelling out their electrostatic effects. The higher the salt concentration, the more screening, and the less a charge-changing mutation actually matters. We implemented this using Debye-Hückel theory (Tanford, 1961): 

Screening length:   kappa (1/nm) = sqrt(I_molar / 0.304)
Screening factor:   exp(−kappa × r_contact)   [r_contact = 0.4 nm]
Score adjustment:   delta_charge × RSA × delta_screening × 0.12

We only apply this correction to surface-exposed residues (estimated RSA > 0.25) that undergo a charge change. Buried charged residues are shielded by the protein's own dielectric and don't respond much to bulk salt concentration. The correction is capped at ±0.15 score units to keep it from dominating the ML prediction.

5.3 Ca²⁺ Binding Correction

This one only applies to cutinase-family enzymes (LCC, TfCut2, Cut190) that have a conserved calcium binding site in their N-terminal region. Calcium coordination contributes significantly to the folding free energy of these enzymes, so mutations that change chelating residues (Asp/Glu) near that site affect stability in a calcium-dependent way. We modelled this with a one-site Hill equation: 

Occupancy:      f = [Ca2+] / (Kd + [Ca2+])     Kd = 0.5 mM
Correction:     −DDG_max × (f_mutant − f_wildtype)     DDG_max = 2.0 kcal/mol

We won't claim this is a perfect model — the actual binding thermodynamics are more complex — but it correctly captures the sign and approximate magnitude of the effect for the residues most relevant to cutinase thermostability.

6. Generating Mutation Candidates

Given a query sequence, we evaluate every possible single amino acid substitution at every non-catalytic position. For a typical 290-residue enzyme like IsPETase, that's roughly 5,500 mutations to score. We exclude the catalytic triad (Ser160, His206, Asp208) because any mutation there would destroy enzyme activity — there's no point predicting stability improvements that break the active site.

From the top single mutations, we build multi-mutant candidates by combining up to six beneficial single mutations. This assumes that ΔΔG contributions are roughly additive across non-interacting residues — a simplification that works reasonably well when the mutation sites are spatially distant, but can break down for nearby residues or those in the same structural element. That's a known limitation we flag in the output.

Each final candidate gets three scores: a stability score from the stacking ensemble, an activity score based on proximity to the catalytic triad (we prefer mutations far from active site residues), and a predicted ΔTm from a separate regressor trained on 4,953 melting temperature measurements from ThermoMutDB (MAE = 4.6 °C, Pearson r = 0.526).

7.2 What the Feature Importances Taught Us

The most useful finding from the feature importance analysis (Figure 3) was that assay conditions — temperature and pH — ranked first and second across the entire ensemble. We were a little surprised by how strongly they dominated. What this tells us is that a significant fraction of the variance in ΔΔG values in our training data comes from the measurement conditions, not just the mutations themselves. Including them as features was the right call, but it also means that predictions made without specifying conditions (e.g., just assuming 25 °C, pH 7) could be considerably off for mutations whose effects are strongly temperature-dependent.

The cross-term features (teal in Figure 3) show up frequently in the top 10, particularly ΔHydrophobicity × temperature and burial × ΔCharge. We took this as confirmation that computing those interactions explicitly — rather than hoping the gradient-boosted trees would discover them — was worthwhile. The burial × ΔCharge feature alone captures the core physics of why charged mutations in hydrophobic cores are so destabilising.

7.3 Limitations

We want to be straightforward about where this model falls short. The biggest gap is the absence of structural information. Methods like FoldX and Rosetta compute explicit van der Waals contacts, hydrogen bond geometries, and solvation energies from 3D coordinates. Our sequence-only RSA and secondary structure estimates are averages — they reflect the statistical tendency of residues at a given type of position, not the actual geometry of any specific protein.

PSSM features (evolutionary conservation profiles from BLAST alignments) are not available in the current deployment. Based on published comparisons of similar models (Montanucci et al., 2019), we estimate these could add 2–4% accuracy. Generating them requires running BLAST searches for each new protein, which takes several minutes per protein — too slow for a real-time web interface. Pre-computing PSSM profiles for common enzyme families is on our list for a future version.

We also can't rule out that some of our accuracy comes from data leakage through the antisymmetry augmentation. If both a forward and reverse mutation from the same protein end up in the same CV fold, the model might learn protein-specific offsets rather than general mutation physics. We took care to deduplicate, but the LOPO CV is the more honest number for generalisation.

8. System and Interface

The back-end is a FastAPI Python service that loads all trained models (six regressors, three classifiers, meta-learner, ΔTm regressor, and feature scaler) into memory at startup. A full optimisation run — exhaustive single-mutation scan followed by multi-mutant candidate assembly and ranking — takes 2–4 seconds on a single CPU.

The front-end is a React web application with a 3Dmol.js interactive 3D molecular viewer showing the IsPETase crystal structure (PDB: 5XJH). Mutation sites are highlighted as green spheres on the backbone. The guided tour animates the camera through each mutation site, and we spent a meaningful amount of time making sure that animation was actually smooth. The fix that worked was baking all geometry (atom positions, bond representations, sphere overlays) to GPU memory once at tour start, then making each subsequent tour step a pure camera move with no geometry rebuild. Calling setStyle() during an active camera animation rebuilds all vertex buffer objects mid-frame, which causes the stutter we were seeing.

9. Conclusion

We built a system that takes a protein sequence, predicts which single amino acid substitutions will improve thermal stability, generates multi-mutant combinations, corrects for real assay conditions, and presents everything in a usable web interface. The model achieves 71.4% binary classification accuracy using only sequence-derived features — no crystal structure, no homology model, no FoldX.

That 71.4% is not the final word. The LOPO cross-validation number (66%) is probably closer to what you'd see on a completely novel protein family. Adding PSSM features and AlphaFold2 predicted structures would likely push accuracy higher. But we think the current model is already useful as a first-pass filter: if you have 10,000 possible mutations and you want to narrow down to 50 for wet lab validation, a model with 71% accuracy can cut the search space dramatically even if it isn't perfect.

The broader goal is to make tools like this accessible to researchers who don't have access to computational biology infrastructure. If a lab working on a new plastic-degrading enzyme can paste in a sequence, set their temperature and salt conditions, and get back a ranked list of candidates in a few seconds, that changes what's feasible for a small research group. That's what we were trying to build.
